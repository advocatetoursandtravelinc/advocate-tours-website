A Journey Back in Time

Few places in Southeast Asia have preserved their colonial past as remarkably as the Ilocos region. Stretching along the northwestern coast of Luzon, Ilocos Norte and Ilocos Sur together form one of the Philippines' most historically rich and scenically diverse destinations. Stone churches, ancestral mansions, cobblestone streets, and windswept dunes sit side by side — each telling a different chapter of the same long story.

The journey to Ilocos rewards those who make the effort. Whether you arrive by bus through the winding mountain roads or fly into Laoag, your first impression of the region is one of timelessness — as though the modern world paused here and decided to let history breathe a little longer.

Vigan City — The Crown Jewel

At the heart of Ilocos Sur lies Vigan City, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the best-preserved examples of Spanish colonial town planning in Asia. Walking down Calle Crisologo — its famous cobblestone street flanked by two-storey ancestral houses with wooden-shuttered windows and stone arches — feels like stepping into the 18th century. Horse-drawn carriages called kalesas clatter along the same stones that Spanish merchants once walked, and the scent of antique wood mingles with the aroma of fresh empanada frying nearby.

Beyond the famous street, Vigan rewards exploration. The Mestizo District hides family-run pottery workshops, weaving studios producing the famous abel cloth, and small museums tucked inside century-old homes. The Plaza Salcedo lights up at night with a fountain show, while the Burgos Museum tells the story of Father José Burgos, a martyr of the Philippine Revolution.

Paoay Church & the Baroque Trail

Paoay Church in Ilocos Norte is one of the Philippines' most dramatic structures — a massive earthquake-baroque fortress of a church built in 1710, its thick buttressed walls rising from the earth like something between a cathedral and a castle. Also a UNESCO World Heritage Site, it stands as a testament to both Spanish ambition and Filipino craftsmanship. Nearby, the Sinking Bell Tower of Laoag leans slightly into the ground, a quirky reminder that even stone structures bow to time.

La Paz Sand Dunes

Just outside Laoag City, the La Paz Sand Dunes surprise every first-time visitor. These are not gentle beach dunes — they are towering mounds of fine sand stretching over 85 square kilometers, rising up to 30 meters high and rolling dramatically toward the sea. The dunes are best experienced at sunrise or golden hour, when the shifting light carves deep shadows across the ridgelines. Thrill-seekers can try 4x4 sandboarding, while those who prefer a quieter experience can simply walk to the shore and watch the South China Sea glitter beyond the sand.

Bangui Windmills & Pagudpud Beaches

Drive north from Laoag and you'll encounter one of the Philippines' most iconic modern landmarks: the Bangui Wind Farm, a row of 20 giant white turbines standing along a black sand beach with the Cordillera mountains behind them. It is a striking, almost surreal sight — industrial scale set against raw natural beauty. Continuing north brings you to Pagudpud, home to the turquoise coves of Blue Lagoon and Saud Beach, often called the Boracay of the North for their crystalline waters and fine white sand.

Ilocano Cuisine — A Food Lover's Paradise

Ilocos is widely regarded as one of the great food regions of the Philippines. Bagnet — deep-fried crispy pork belly — is the local answer to lechon, and locals eat it with a sour fermented sauce called sukang Iloko. The Ilocos empanada is entirely its own creation: a bright orange, crispy rice-flour shell filled with grated green papaya, longganisa sausage, and egg, sold sizzling hot from roadside stalls. Pinakbet, the country's most famous vegetable dish, is believed to have originated here, as did the sweet, dense tupig rice cake wrapped in banana leaf and grilled over charcoal.

Practical Tips

Ilocos is best explored over three to four days. A hired van or car is recommended to cover the distance between the Ilocos Sur heritage sites and the northern beaches and dunes of Ilocos Norte comfortably. The dry season from November to April offers the most reliable weather, though Vigan's streets are beautiful year-round. Accommodations range from restored heritage mansions in Vigan to beachfront resorts in Pagudpud.
