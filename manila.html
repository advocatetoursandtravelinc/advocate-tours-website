Where Every Philippine Journey Begins

Manila is almost always the first place international visitors set foot in the Philippines — and too often it is treated as merely a transit point, a city to pass through on the way to beaches and islands. That is a mistake. Manila is one of Southeast Asia's most historically layered and culturally rich capitals, a city where you can walk streets that have witnessed four centuries of colonial rule, two world wars, a dictator's fall, and the relentless reinvention of a people who have never stopped finding joy in the midst of difficulty. Give Manila even a day or two of genuine attention, and it will reward you generously.

The city that most visitors experience is actually Metro Manila — a conurbation of 16 cities home to over 14 million people, sprawling across the shores of Manila Bay. Within this vast urban landscape, two places stand above all others for the international traveler: Intramuros, the original walled colonial city, and Rizal Park, the national heart of the Philippines.

Intramuros — The Walled City

Built by the Spanish in 1571 on the southern bank of the Pasig River, Intramuros — literally "within the walls" — was for three centuries the political, religious, and military center of Spanish colonial power in Asia. Its thick stone walls, up to five meters wide and six meters high, enclosed an entire city of churches, convents, government buildings, schools, and the mansions of the colonial elite. Today those walls still stand, encircling about 64 hectares of Manila's oldest district, and walking within them is to step into a different time.

The most important building within Intramuros is San Agustin Church, completed in 1607 and the oldest stone church in the Philippines. It is also a UNESCO World Heritage Site — one of the four Baroque Churches of the Philippines recognized for their exceptional architectural value. Its interior is extraordinary: trompe l'oeil ceiling paintings that create an illusion of soaring height, carved mahogany choir stalls brought from Mexico, and a collection of religious art spanning four centuries. The church survived the devastation of World War II that destroyed almost everything else in Intramuros — a miracle that locals attribute to divine protection.

Adjacent to the church, the Casa Manila Museum recreates a wealthy colonial household of the 19th century with period furniture, imported porcelain, and ancestral portraits, giving visitors a vivid sense of how Manila's elite lived during the Spanish era. Outside its doors, Plaza San Luis Complex is a reconstructed colonial square of shops, restaurants, and galleries built in the Spanish-Filipino style, pleasant for an afternoon wander.

Fort Santiago

At the northern tip of Intramuros, commanding the mouth of the Pasig River, stands Fort Santiago — the most historically resonant site in Manila. Built originally by the Spanish conquistador Miguel López de Legazpi in 1571, the fort served as the seat of colonial military power for over 300 years. During World War II, Japanese forces used its dungeons to imprison Filipino and American soldiers, many of whom died of starvation and disease in its underground cells that flooded at high tide.

But Fort Santiago is remembered above all as the place where José Rizal — the Philippines' national hero — spent the final hours of his life before his execution by the Spanish on December 30, 1896. Rizal's cell has been preserved as a shrine, and his footsteps are literally embedded in the pavement along the route he walked to his execution at what is now Rizal Park. The fort's gardens, shaded by old acacia trees, are a surprisingly peaceful place to sit and reflect on the extraordinary weight of Philippine history that echoes through this ground.

Rizal Park — The Nation's Heartbeat

Rizal Park — formally Luneta Park, but universally known by the name of the hero whose monument anchors it — is the symbolic center of the Philippine nation. Stretching 58 hectares along Manila Bay at the edge of Ermita district, it is where Filipinos gather for national celebrations, where the President reads the State of the Nation address, and where ordinary Manila families spread picnic blankets on weekend afternoons to watch their children run across the wide lawns. It is a park that belongs to everyone, and its democratic, unhurried atmosphere is one of the most pleasant things about it.

At its heart stands the Rizal Monument — a tall obelisk topped by a bronze statue of José Rizal, flanked by an eternal flame and guarded around the clock by ceremonial sentries from the Philippine military. Beneath the monument, in a marble crypt, lie Rizal's remains. The monument is the starting point of every Philippine Independence Day celebration and the most visited site in the country.

Beyond the monument, the park contains a Chinese garden and Japanese garden reflecting Manila's deep Asian connections, an open-air auditorium where free concerts are held on weekend evenings, a children's playground, and a series of historical markers tracing key moments in Philippine history. At the park's western edge, facing Manila Bay, a wide promenade offers one of the Philippines' most famous views: the Manila Bay sunset, when the sun sinks into the sea in shades of gold, orange, and rose, silhouetting the distant mountains of Bataan. It is a view that Filipinos have been watching for generations, and it never loses its power.

Binondo — The World's Oldest Chinatown

Just across the Jones Bridge from Intramuros lies Binondo, established in 1594 as a settlement for Chinese traders and widely considered the world's oldest Chinatown. Today it remains a living, working district of Chinese-Filipino commerce, religion, and above all, food. A food walk through Binondo — guided or self-directed — is one of the great culinary adventures of Manila: stalls and hole-in-the-wall restaurants serve dishes that blend Fujian Chinese cooking with Filipino flavors, including pancit noodles, siopao buns, tikoy rice cakes, and the celebrated fried siopao of Presidente restaurant, unchanged for decades.

The National Museum Complex

Facing Rizal Park from the north side of Padre Burgos Avenue, the National Museum of the Philippines comprises three magnificent neoclassical buildings — the Museum of Fine Arts, the Museum of Natural History, and the Museum of Anthropology. Together they house the country's most important collections: the great paintings of Juan Luna and Fernando Amorsolo, the bones of the Tabon Man (one of the oldest human remains found in Asia), and the extraordinary collection of the San Diego shipwreck artifacts. Admission is free, and the buildings themselves — restored to their Beaux-Arts grandeur — are as impressive as what they contain.

Practical Tips

Manila is best explored by hired car or taxi — the city's traffic is heavy and distances are deceptive. Intramuros, Rizal Park, Binondo, and the National Museum can all be visited comfortably in a single full day. The coolest and most pleasant months are November through February. For those with an early-morning flight, an overnight stay near the airport in Pasay allows for a quick Intramuros and Rizal Park morning before departure. Grab-hailing apps (Grab) are the easiest way to get around and are widely available throughout the city.
