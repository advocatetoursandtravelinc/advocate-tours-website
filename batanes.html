The Edge of the Philippines

Batanes is unlike anywhere else in the Philippines — or in Asia, for that matter. Perched at the very northern tip of the archipelago, closer to Taiwan than to Manila, this remote group of ten small islands sits at the crossroads of two great oceans. The landscape is breathtaking in a way that feels almost European: vast rolling grasslands dotted with stone houses, dramatic cliff edges dropping into churning seas, and lighthouses standing guard over empty roads. Yet the warmth of the Ivatan people and the flavors of their cooking remind you unmistakably that you are in the Philippines.

Batanes receives relatively few tourists compared to other Philippine destinations — and that is precisely its greatest gift. Here, you can cycle quiet roads, eat at a family-run honesty café where you pay whatever you think is fair, and watch the sun sink into the sea from a cliff edge without another tourist in sight.

The Three Main Islands

Batan Island is where you arrive, home to the provincial capital Basco and most of the island's accommodations and restaurants. From Batan you can reach the iconic viewpoints of Naidi Hills — topped by a picturesque lighthouse — and the famous Marlboro Country (officially Racuh A Payaman), a sweeping plateau of grassland where horses roam freely above a dramatic coastline. The hills here are so lush and cinematic that visitors consistently describe it as one of the most beautiful views they have ever seen.

Sabtang Island, reached by a short but sometimes wave-tossed boat ride, is the cultural heart of Batanes. Its villages of Chavayan and Savidug are living heritage sites where Ivatan stone houses — built from limestone and cogon grass to withstand the powerful typhoons that sweep through each year — have stood for centuries. Walking through these stone-walled lanes, where roosters crow and elderly women weave vakul palm hats, is one of the most moving cultural experiences the Philippines has to offer.

Itbayat Island, the largest and most remote of the three, is for serious adventurers only. There is no proper port — passengers are hoisted onto the island by crane — and the interior is a rugged landscape of forests, caves, and ancient village ruins. Those who make the effort are rewarded with absolute solitude.

Ivatan Culture & the Honesty Store

The Ivatan people are among the most distinctive cultural groups in the Philippines, with their own language, architecture, and way of life shaped by centuries of isolation and the ever-present force of the sea and storms. One of Batanes' most famous institutions is the Honesty Coffee Shop in Ivana — an unmanned roadside store where you take what you need, record it in a notebook, and leave your payment in a tin can. It has been running this way for decades, and it almost never loses money. That tells you everything you need to know about Batanes.

Landscapes & Natural Wonders

Batanes rewards those who simply wander. The Vayang Rolling Hills on the western coast of Batan offer panoramic views of the sea and distant islands. The Valugan Boulder Beach is covered in perfectly rounded volcanic boulders — black and smooth — that were hurled ashore by past eruptions of Mt. Iraya, the active volcano that dominates northern Batan. Morong Beach, shaped like a crescent moon, is one of the most photographed spots in the province, with its white sand and rough surf framed by dramatic cliffs.

Ivatan Cuisine

Batanes cuisine is hearty, simple, and utterly unique. Uvud balls — made from the pith of banana trunk mixed with pork or fish — are the local staple, often served in a ginger broth. Coconut crab (tatus), the world's largest land crab, is a delicacy found only here and in a few remote Pacific islands. Lunis is sun-dried pork preserved without refrigeration — a centuries-old technique developed by people who needed to store food through months of storm season. Wash it all down with palek, a mildly alcoholic sugarcane wine that locals share freely with visitors.

Practical Tips

Batanes is accessible only by plane from Manila or Tuguegarao. Flights are weather-dependent and can be cancelled, so build flexibility into your itinerary. The best travel window is March to May, before typhoon season begins. Accommodations are simple but comfortable — most are family-run guesthouses where home-cooked meals are included. Cash is essential as ATM availability is limited. A minimum of four to five days is recommended to explore Batan and take a day trip to Sabtang.
