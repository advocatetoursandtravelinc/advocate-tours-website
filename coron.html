The Diver's Paradise

Coron occupies a unique position in the Philippine travel landscape: it is simultaneously one of the country's most photogenic island-hopping destinations and one of the world's most acclaimed wreck diving locations. The dramatic karst limestone islands of the Calamian Archipelago, rising sheer from mirror-flat bays, create a seascape that looks almost staged in its beauty. Beneath the surface, some of the most intact and marine-life-rich shipwrecks in Asia lie waiting at accessible depths. Whether you come to dive or simply to drift between hidden lagoons on a wooden bangka, Coron will rank among the most beautiful places you have ever visited.

Kayangan Lake — Asia's Clearest

Often described as the cleanest lake in Asia, Kayangan Lake sits inside Coron Island, accessed by a short but steep staircase climb over a limestone ridge from the sea. The view from the top of that ridge — looking down into the lake's mirror surface, framed by towering cliffs and jungle — is one of the most iconic photographs in Philippine travel. In the water itself, visibility extends to over ten meters in every direction, and the submerged limestone formations visible from the surface are extraordinary. Snorkelers can explore the shallows where freshwater and saltwater mix, creating thermoclines visible as shimmering layers in the water. Scuba divers find the underwater cliff system equally dramatic.

Twin Lagoon

Also on Coron Island, Twin Lagoon consists of two connected bodies of water separated by a low limestone wall that can be swum through or climbed over. The outer lagoon opens to the sea, while the inner lagoon — enclosed entirely by towering cliffs — is calmer, warmer, and otherworldly in its isolation. The water in the inner lagoon is a layered mix of salt and fresh water, creating a thermocline that snorkelers can feel passing through: warm above, cold below. The cliffs that surround it block the wind entirely, and the silence inside — broken only by the lapping of water and distant birdsong — is extraordinary.

WWII Japanese Shipwrecks

On September 24, 1944, American aircraft attacked and sank a Japanese naval fleet sheltering in Coron Bay during World War II. Today, twelve of those Japanese warships lie at depths of between 10 and 40 meters off the coast of Busuanga, and they have become some of the most celebrated wreck dives in the world. Covered in soft and hard corals, inhabited by enormous schools of glassfish, lionfish, sea turtles, and occasional reef sharks, these wrecks are extraordinary both as historical artifacts and as thriving artificial reefs. The most famous include the Okikawa Maru, an oil tanker of immense scale, and the Olympia Maru, a cargo ship whose holds can be safely penetrated by recreational divers.

Barracuda Lake

Barracuda Lake, also on Coron Island, is one of the most unusual dive sites in the world. A saltwater lake accessible by a short rocky climb from the sea, it features a dramatic thermocline where the water temperature shifts suddenly from cool at the surface to an eerie 38°C at depth — the result of geothermal heating from below. The transition is disorienting and fascinating, and visibility in the warm lower layer is surreal. The lake is named for the large barracuda that inhabit it, though they are generally harmless curiosities rather than threats.

Island Hopping & Beaches

For non-divers, Coron's island-hopping tours offer access to some of the most beautiful beaches in the Philippines. Malcapuya Island has a long crescent of white sand and coral gardens just meters from shore. Banana Island is a quiet, palm-fringed retreat with clear water and a relaxed family atmosphere. Siete Pecados Marine Park, a cluster of seven limestone outcrops near Coron Town, is one of the best snorkeling sites in the area, with shallow coral gardens alive with hundreds of species of reef fish. Each island-hopping tour visits a different combination of these spots, and most include a freshly cooked lunch served on the beach.

Mt. Tapyas & Coron Town

Overlooking Coron Town, Mt. Tapyas is climbed by a staircase of 724 steps — a workout rewarded by panoramic views of the entire bay, the outlying islands, and on clear days, the distant peaks of the Calamian range. Sunset from the summit is spectacular. Below in Coron Town itself, a small grid of streets lined with dive shops, guesthouses, and restaurants has grown steadily without losing its frontier charm. The town's Maquinit Hot Springs, a series of natural saltwater pools heated by geothermal springs, make an excellent wind-down after a full day on the water.

Practical Tips

Coron is served by direct flights from Manila to Francisco B. Reyes Airport in Busuanga, about 30 minutes from town. The journey from airport to town is by van. Most visitors base themselves in Coron Town and take day trips by bangka. Three to four days allows enough time for both wreck diving and island hopping. Book dive operators in advance during peak season. Live-aboard dive boats also operate out of Coron for multi-day dive expeditions through the Calamian islands.
